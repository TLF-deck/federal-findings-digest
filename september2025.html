Dear Colleague Letter
Ed-Flex Authority: State Waiver of Federal Requirements Without Prior ED Approval
Office of Elementary and Secondary Education · September 17, 2025
What it says

OESE sent a Dear Colleague Letter to all chief state school officers informing them that states may apply for Ed-Flex authority under the Education Flexibility Partnership Act of 1999. States with Ed-Flex authority can waive certain federal requirements under Title I Parts A, C, and D; Title II Part A; Title IV Part A; and Perkins, without submitting individual waiver requests to ED for approval. Specific waivers available include the 15 percent carryover limitation for Title I funds. As of the 2023-2024 school year, 11 states hold Ed-Flex authority, and those states reported granting approximately 575 waivers that year. This is the sixth flexibility guidance the current administration has issued on expanding education choice and flexibility under ESEA.

TLF Interpretation
Ed-Flex authority allows approved SEAs to waive certain ESEA statutory and regulatory requirements for LEAs without prior federal approval. For districts in Ed-Flex states, compliance obligations may differ if the SEA has granted local waivers. The 15 percent Title I carryover limitation is a specific example: a waiver could provide districts with more flexibility in budget planning across fiscal years. Federal programs directors must confirm whether their state holds Ed-Flex authority and monitor state-level waiver announcements. A waiver does not eliminate the underlying statutory goal; it substitutes a waived requirement with a locally designed alternative that must still advance the program's objectives. Maintain documentation of any waivers in effect and the alternative approaches in place.
Your Action
  • Confirm whether your state holds Ed-Flex authority. If not, this guidance has no direct compliance impact on your district at this time.
  • If your state holds Ed-Flex authority, contact your SEA to determine whether any waivers have been granted affecting your Title I, Title II, Title IV, or Perkins programs.
  • For any active Ed-Flex waivers, document the waiver, the requirement waived, the alternative approach your district is implementing, and evidence that the alternative advances the program's statutory objectives.
  • Monitor whether additional states apply for Ed-Flex authority in response to this guidance. Eleven states currently hold authority; that number may grow following this reminder.
Primary source: Dear Colleague Letter on Ed-Flex, OESE, September 17, 2025 (PDF) ↗

ED Grants
Federal Register Notice
EIR Expansion Grants: Absolute Priority on Evidence-Based Literacy (Application Deadline Passed)
U.S. Department of Education · September 12, 2025 · Application deadline: October 14, 2025 (closed)
What it says

ED announced FY 2025 Education Innovation and Research (EIR) Expansion Grants with an absolute priority on promoting evidence-based literacy aligned to the science of reading, drawn from the Supplemental Priorities published September 9, 2025 (90 FR 43514). Two competitive preference priorities include returning education decision-making to states. Applications were available September 12, 2025, with a deadline of October 14, 2025. Grantees must serve high-need students and conduct an independent evaluation. The competition reflects ED's policy direction toward literacy instruction and state autonomy and signals grant competition criteria likely to recur in future discretionary programs.

TLF Interpretation
The October 14 application deadline has passed. This entry is included for its policy signal value: the absolute priority on science-of-reading aligned literacy instruction is now embedded in the Supplemental Priorities framework that governs all ED discretionary grant competitions. Districts developing literacy programs or seeking future competitive grant funding should align with this framework. The competitive preference for returning education to states also signals that proposals emphasizing local and state-level decision-making will be advantaged in future competitions across multiple program areas.
Your Action
  • Review whether your district's current literacy programs are grounded in science-of-reading evidence bases. Alignment with this framework is increasingly a prerequisite for competitive federal grant eligibility, not just a best practice.
  • Review the Supplemental Priorities published September 9, 2025 (90 FR 43514) for the definitions and frameworks that will govern future ED discretionary competitions. These priorities now shape scoring criteria across Title I, Title III, and other competitive programs.
  • If your district applied and was not selected, request reviewer feedback. The absolute priority criteria are now well-defined and feedback will inform future submissions.
Source: Federal Register, 90 FR 44179, September 12, 2025 ↗ · Supplemental Priorities: 90 FR 43514, September 9, 2025 ↗ · ESEA Title IV Part E (EIR program)
ED Policy
ED Policy
Federal Register: Final Priorities
Secretary's Supplemental Priorities and Definitions for Discretionary Grants Programs
U.S. Department of Education · September 9, 2025 · 90 FR 43514
What it says

ED published final Supplemental Priorities and Definitions for Discretionary Grants Programs at 90 FR 43514, replacing the December 10, 2021 supplemental priorities. The three final priorities are: Evidence-Based Literacy (aligned to the science of reading), Educational Choice, and Returning Education to the States. These priorities apply across ED discretionary grant programs and are incorporated as absolute or competitive preference priorities in subsequent grant competitions, including the EIR Expansion Grants announced September 12. They affect Title I, Title III, Title IV, and other ESEA programs where discretionary competitions occur.

TLF Interpretation
These Supplemental Priorities now govern the scoring and eligibility framework for all ED discretionary grant competitions going forward. Federal programs directors preparing grant applications for any ED discretionary program must align proposals with these three priorities and incorporate the definitions from the September 9 notice. The shift from the 2021 priorities is significant: equity and access framing has been replaced by science-of-reading literacy, school choice, and state autonomy framing. Grant proposals that relied on prior administration priority language should be revised before submission to any future competition.
Your Action
  • Read 90 FR 43514 in full before preparing any future ED discretionary grant application. The definitions in this notice are operationally binding in competitions that incorporate these priorities.
  • Audit your existing grant applications and program narratives for language drawn from the 2021 supplemental priorities. Proposals using prior-administration framing will be at a competitive disadvantage and may fail to meet absolute priority criteria.
  • Brief your grant writers and curriculum staff on the science-of-reading definition as operationalized in this notice. Vague references to "evidence-based literacy" are insufficient; alignment must be demonstrable against the specific framework.
  • Note that additional supplemental priorities have been proposed since September 9, including Artificial Intelligence (proposed July 21), Patriotic Education (proposed September 17), Career Pathways and Workforce Readiness (proposed September 25), and Meaningful Learning Opportunities (finalized February 2026). Monitor the Federal Register for any that are finalized and incorporated into programs you apply to.
Source: Federal Register, 90 FR 43514, September 9, 2025 ↗ · 20 U.S.C. §1221e-3 (Secretary's general authority) · 20 U.S.C. §3474

Perkins
Guidance
FY 2026 Perkins State Plan Revisions and Budget Submission Requirements
U.S. Department of Education, Office of Career, Technical, and Adult Education · September 2025
What it says

OCTAE announced requirements and instructions for submission of Perkins V State Plan Revisions and Budgets for FY 2026 Grant Awards. States administering Perkins must submit plan revisions following the four-year planning cycle, with many states submitting new or amended plans for FY 2026. Perkins V funds career and technical education programs at approximately $1.4 billion annually. Compliance with FY 2026 state plan submissions affects local subgrant allocations, performance indicators, and program requirements for districts receiving Perkins funds.

TLF Interpretation
Districts receiving Perkins subgrants must align local applications and CTE programs with state plan priorities and performance measures established in the FY 2026 state submission. CTE directors and federal programs staff should monitor state-level plan revisions to understand how changes in state priorities, performance indicators, or funding formulas may affect local allocations. Any state plan modifications must be reflected in local Perkins applications, budgets, and accountability data systems. Note also that the ED-DOL workforce development integration announced in November 2025 may affect Perkins plan submission procedures; contact your state CTE office for current guidance on any portal or process changes.
Your Action
  • Contact your state CTE office to obtain the FY 2026 local application guidance and confirm whether the state plan revision has been submitted and approved.
  • Review your district's current CTE program design against the state's FY 2026 plan priorities and performance indicators. Misalignment between local programs and the state plan is a monitoring flag.
  • Confirm your Perkins accountability data systems are capturing the performance measures required under the FY 2026 state plan. Data gaps discovered during monitoring are harder to remediate than gaps identified proactively.
  • If your state has integrated Perkins into the WIOA portal following the November 2025 ED-DOL partnership, confirm local submission processes and deadlines have not changed.
Source: Perkins V (20 U.S.C. §2301 et seq.) · 34 CFR Part 99
Note: A specific URL for the FY 2026 Perkins state plan revision guidance was not available at time of publication. Verify current requirements at cte.ed.gov ↗ or by contacting your state CTE office directly.

OCR / Title VI
Resolution Agreement (Terminated April 1, 2025)
OCR Resolution Agreement with Rapid City Area School District (SD) on Title VI Racial Discrimination in Discipline and Advanced Learning
Office for Civil Rights · Agreement entered May 29, 2024 · September 2025 deadlines active at time of issue · Terminated April 1, 2025
Post-publication update: This resolution agreement was terminated by OCR on April 1, 2025, following President Trump's Executive Order "Ending Illegal Discrimination and Restoring Merit-Based Opportunity," which directed federal agencies to eliminate race-based policies. The agreement's September 2025 implementation deadlines were active when this digest was compiled, but the agreement itself no longer has legal force. It is included here for the signal value of the underlying findings and as context for districts monitoring OCR's Title VI enforcement posture.
What it says

OCR entered a resolution agreement with Rapid City Area School District in South Dakota on May 29, 2024, following a compliance review opened in 2010. OCR's investigation found that Native American students were disciplined at rates 2.06 times higher than white students for all discipline referrals in 2021-22, and 4.57 to 5.84 times higher for specific categories including suspensions, truancy referrals, law enforcement referrals, and arrests. OCR also found discriminatory barriers in access to advanced learning programs. The agreement required policy revisions by February 2025, hiring of a discipline equity supervisor and advanced learning coordinator, establishment of a Stakeholder Equity Committee, staff training, and annual September reporting on implementation. The agreement was terminated April 1, 2025.

TLF Interpretation
The Rapid City agreement's termination reflects a shift in OCR's Title VI enforcement posture under the current administration. However, the underlying legal obligation under Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 remains unchanged: districts may not discriminate on the basis of race, color, or national origin. The statistical disparities documented in Rapid City (2.06x overall referral rate, up to 5.84x for arrests) represent the kind of data pattern that can trigger OCR complaints regardless of the current enforcement environment. Districts with significant racial or ethnic enrollment disparities in discipline data should treat that data as a compliance risk, not only as a legal obligation when a resolution agreement is in force.
Your Action
  • Pull your district's discipline data disaggregated by race and ethnicity. Calculate referral rate ratios across student groups for all discipline categories. Ratios above 1.5x for any group in any category warrant internal review and documentation of root cause analysis.
  • Review your district's advanced learning program enrollment data by race and ethnicity. Significant underrepresentation of any racial group in honors, AP, or gifted programming is a Title VI risk regardless of current OCR enforcement activity.
  • Confirm that your district has a functioning internal process for reviewing discipline disparities. OCR's ability to enforce agreements has varied by administration, but Title VI private rights of action remain available to affected students and families independent of OCR enforcement.
  • Brief your civil rights coordinator and legal counsel on the termination of this agreement and the underlying disparity data. The risk to the district has not been eliminated; only the federal agreement mechanism has been removed.
Source: OCR Resolution Agreement, Case No. 07-11-5001, May 29, 2024 ↗ · Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (42 U.S.C. §2000d) · Executive Order "Ending Illegal Discrimination and Restoring Merit-Based Opportunity" (January 20, 2025)
OCR / Section 504
Resolution Agreement
OCR Resolution Agreement with Sumter School District (SC) on Section 504 and Title II Compliance
Office for Civil Rights · Agreement entered August 26, 2025 · Case No. 11-25-1375
What it says

OCR entered a resolution agreement with Sumter School District in South Carolina on August 26, 2025 (Case No. 11-25-1375), addressing Section 504 and Title II of the ADA compliance. The agreement requires the district to convene a group by September 30, 2025 to determine whether compensatory services are appropriate for a student who may not have received FAPE between the beginning of the 2024-2025 school year and January 27, 2025. The district must also submit training materials for OCR approval and conduct training for all relevant staff on Section 504 and Title II obligations. A documentation report on compensatory services provided is due to OCR by December 18, 2025.

TLF Interpretation
This agreement addresses a failure to provide timely and appropriate Section 504 services to a student during a portion of a school year, with OCR requiring compensatory services as a remedy. The compensatory services obligation signals that OCR will hold districts accountable for service delivery gaps retroactively, not just prospectively. Section 504 coordinators and special education directors should review procedures for determining eligibility, developing accommodation plans, and monitoring service delivery to ensure students receive required supports without delay. Documentation of service delivery is as important as the delivery itself; undocumented services are treated the same as undelivered services during an OCR review.
Your Action
  • Review your district's procedures for monitoring Section 504 plan implementation. Confirm that service delivery is documented in real time, not reconstructed after the fact.
  • Audit a sample of active Section 504 plans for evidence of actual service delivery. A plan on file without documentation of implementation is a liability during any OCR review.
  • Confirm your district has a process for identifying and addressing Section 504 service gaps mid-year, before they accumulate into a compensatory services obligation. Gaps identified proactively are far less costly than those identified by OCR.
  • Verify that Section 504 training for relevant staff is current. The Sumter agreement requires OCR-approved training as a remedy; having current training documentation in place makes this a non-issue if your district is ever subject to a complaint.
Source: OCR Resolution Agreement, Case No. 11-25-1375, August 26, 2025 ↗ · Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (29 U.S.C. §794) · Title II of the ADA (42 U.S.C. §12132) · 34 CFR Part 104

OIG
Semiannual Report to Congress
OIG Semiannual Report to Congress No. 91: April through September 2025
Office of Inspector General, U.S. Department of Education · Report period April 1 through September 30, 2025
What it says

OIG released its statutory semiannual report covering April 1 through September 30, 2025, presenting audit and investigative activities to identify and stop fraud, waste, and abuse in Department programs. The report covers K-12 formula grant oversight, state accountability system implementation, and pandemic relief fund audits. As documented in the December 2025 issue of this digest, OIG also reported that 247 OCR staff were on administrative leave from March through mid-December 2025, with the cost estimated at $28.5 million to $38 million (a figure later confirmed by a GAO report). OIG continued oversight of ESSER spending as closeout deadlines approached.

TLF Interpretation
OIG's semiannual report period covers the same window when OCR's enforcement capacity was severely diminished due to staffing disruptions. Districts should review the report for audit findings and case studies in their program areas. The continued ESSER oversight signal is particularly important: OIG indicated it would maintain focus on pandemic relief fund spending through and beyond the 2025 closeout period. Questioned costs from ESSER audits remain a live risk regardless of whether grant periods have ended. Documentation practices during the grant period are the primary defense against questioned costs identified in post-closeout audits.
Your Action
  • Review OIG Semiannual Report No. 91 for audit findings related to your program areas, particularly K-12 formula grants and ESSER. Common findings in the report are strong indicators of what OIG considers high risk.
  • Confirm your district's ESSER closeout documentation is complete and retained. Audit risk does not end at the grant closeout date; OIG can and does conduct post-closeout reviews.
  • Review internal controls for formula grant administration against the risk areas OIG identified during this period. Internal control weaknesses identified in other districts' audits frequently recur across similar-sized and similar-context districts.
  • Brief your CFO and federal programs director on the report's key findings. Document that review as evidence of active compliance monitoring.
Source: Inspector General Act of 1978 (semiannual report requirement) · OIG reports at oversight.gov ↗
