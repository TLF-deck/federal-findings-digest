This represents a significant shift in how districts will interact with federal programs, though ED assured grantees they should not expect programmatic disruptions. The administration's focus on workforce development integration and reducing federal bureaucracy is reshaping the compliance landscape.

ED Announces Six New Interagency Agreements to Transfer Programs to DOL, DOI, HHS, and State
U.S. Department of Education · November 18, 2025
What it says

ED announced six new interagency agreements with four agencies — DOL, DOI, HHS, and State — to transfer administration of certain education programs. DOL will take on a greater role in administering federal K-12 programs to better align them with workforce and college programs, managing competitions, providing technical assistance, and integrating ED programs with employment and training programs DOL already administers. DOI will administer Indian Education programs. HHS will administer the CCAMPIS child care program and foreign medical accreditation. State will administer Fulbright-Hays international education grants. ED stated that states and grantees should not expect programmatic disruptions and that ED maintains all statutory responsibilities for policymaking and oversight under each agreement.

TLF Interpretation
The six interagency agreements transfer day-to-day administration of specified ED programs to other agencies while ED retains statutory authority and oversight. For K-12 districts, the most consequential is the Elementary and Secondary Education Partnership with DOL, which gives DOL a greater role in administering federal K-12 programs including competitions and technical assistance. Although ED has assured grantees of no programmatic disruptions, any transfer of program administration creates risk of changed guidance, reporting requirements, or monitoring priorities during transition. Federal programs directors must monitor communications from both ED and DOL for any transition-related changes affecting grant programs, submission portals, or compliance expectations.
Your Action
  • Identify which of your district's federal programs fall under the ED-DOL Elementary and Secondary Education Partnership. Confirm current grant contacts and confirm whether any submission portals or reporting channels have changed.
  • Monitor both ED and DOL communications channels for transition-related guidance. Do not assume that guidance from one agency is sufficient — both agencies are now part of program administration.
  • Document all communications received regarding program transitions. If guidance from DOL contradicts prior ED guidance, contact both agencies for clarification and document the discrepancy before taking action.
  • Brief your superintendent and board on the restructuring. The shift to DOL administration of K-12 programs is material enough to warrant board-level awareness, particularly for districts with significant federal program budgets.
Source: ED Press Release, November 18, 2025 ↗ · Economy Act (31 U.S.C. §1535) · Elementary and Secondary Education Act · IDEA · Carl D. Perkins CTE Act

OSEP Releases Fast Facts Report on 50 Years of IDEA
U.S. Department of Education, Office of Special Education Programs · November 2025
What it says

OSEP published Fast Facts commemorating 50 years of IDEA, providing historical context and current national implementation data. The report summarizes key facts related to data collection authorized by IDEA Section 618, including child count, educational environments, discipline, and exiting data. While primarily retrospective, the publication provides baseline national data districts can use for comparing their special education programs and outcomes to national trends.

TLF Interpretation
OSEP's retrospective Fast Facts report does not impose new requirements, but it offers national baseline data on placement rates, discipline, and outcomes that state monitoring agencies and federal reviewers will have access to. Districts whose LRE placement rates, discipline rates for students with disabilities, or exit data deviate significantly from national patterns should treat that deviation as a monitoring flag. Special education directors should review the report's data and assess whether district practices align with or diverge from national patterns, particularly in areas that are common compliance review targets: least restrictive environment placement rates and disproportionate discipline of students with disabilities.
Your Action
  • Review the OSEP Fast Facts data on educational environments. Compare your district's LRE placement percentages against the national data. Flag significant divergences for documentation and review.
  • Review discipline data for students with disabilities. If your district's rates of removal, suspension, or alternative placement for students with disabilities exceed national norms, document the justification and confirm procedural safeguard compliance.
  • Confirm your IDEA Section 618 data reporting is accurate and current. The national data in this report originates from district submissions — inaccurate local data affects both your compliance record and the national baseline.
  • Share the report with your special education director as a baseline reference for program self-assessment before the next state monitoring cycle.
Source: OSEP Fast Facts, 50 Years of IDEA, November 2025 ↗ · IDEA Section 618 (20 U.S.C. §1418) · 34 CFR Part 300
Note: The OSEP Fast Facts page links to multiple reports. Navigate to the 50th anniversary Fast Facts specifically for the November 2025 publication.

DOL Issues WIOA Waiver Guidance with Recommendations for Integrating Perkins CTE Programs
U.S. Department of Labor, Employment and Training Administration · November 25, 2025
What it says

DOL issued a Training and Employment Guidance Letter (TEGL) to governors and workforce boards in collaboration with ED's Office of Career, Technical, and Adult Education regarding available WIOA waiver and flexibility options. The guidance included recommendations for further integrating Perkins career and technical education programs into the public workforce system. States are being encouraged to combine WIOA and Perkins state plans into a single portal, potentially affecting local application timelines and coordination requirements with workforce development boards.

TLF Interpretation
This DOL TEGL, issued in collaboration with ED's OCTAE, encourages states to integrate Perkins V CTE programs into WIOA workforce systems including combining state plans into a single portal. Districts administering Perkins-funded CTE programs should anticipate potential changes to local application timelines, coordination requirements with workforce boards, and state-level planning processes. The guidance operates at the state level — its direct effect on districts will depend on how each state responds. Federal programs directors must monitor state CTE communications for any modifications to Perkins V local application procedures, submission deadlines, or reporting requirements resulting from WIOA integration.
Your Action
  • Contact your state CTE office to determine whether the state plans to respond to this TEGL by combining Perkins and WIOA state plans and whether any changes to local application procedures are anticipated.
  • Review your current Perkins application timeline and local needs assessment process. If the state combines portals or planning timelines, local submission deadlines may shift.
  • Document current coordination activities with your local workforce development board. The guidance increases federal expectations for cross-system collaboration — undocumented coordination is a gap that will surface during monitoring.
  • Ensure CTE program staff are aware of the potential for procedural changes and are monitoring state communications.
Source: DOL/ETA Training and Employment Guidance Letter, November 25, 2025 · Perkins V (20 U.S.C. §2301 et seq.) · WIOA (29 U.S.C. §3101 et seq.)
Note: A direct URL to the specific TEGL was not available at time of publication. The guidance is documented in the ED/DOL December 5, 2025 press release at ed.gov ↗. Verify the TEGL directly at DOL ETA advisories ↗.

Press Release / Interagency Coordination
ED Announces Plans to Integrate Perkins State Plan Portal into WIOA System
U.S. Department of Education, Office of Career, Technical, and Adult Education · November 18, 2025
What it says

ED announced that subsequent efforts following the ED-DOL workforce development partnership will focus on integrating the Perkins state plan portal into the WIOA state plan portal so states with combined WIOA plans can submit information through a single portal. ED and DOL are synchronizing the timeline for submitting 2026 WIOA State Plan modifications and will provide joint feedback on submissions. This is a companion announcement to the November 18 interagency agreements press release.

TLF Interpretation
The integration of the Perkins state plan portal into the WIOA portal is a structural change that will affect how states submit Perkins V information and may alter the timing and process for local applications that flow from state plans. The synchronization of 2026 WIOA State Plan modification timelines with Perkins submissions means that any delays or changes at the state level could shift local application and reporting deadlines. Districts should not treat this as a future concern — state-level planning for combined submissions is already underway and will affect FY 2026 local applications.
Your Action
  • Confirm with your state CTE office whether the Perkins portal integration has already changed submission procedures for FY 2026 state plan modifications and whether local application timelines will be affected.
  • Verify that your district's Perkins grant contacts and submission pathways are current. If the state has migrated to DOL's GrantSolutions system, confirm your district has the necessary access and has updated contact information.
  • Flag FY 2026 Perkins local application deadlines for early review. If the state has not yet communicated any changes, request a status update in writing so your district has documentation of the inquiry.
Source: ED Press Release, November 18, 2025 ↗ · Perkins V (20 U.S.C. §2301 et seq.) · WIOA (29 U.S.C. §3101 et seq.)

Criminal Enforcement
Former Boone County Schools (WV) Maintenance Supervisor Sentenced for $3.4 Million Fraud Scheme
Office of Inspector General, U.S. Department of Education · November 20, 2025
What it says

OIG announced the sentencing of a former West Virginia school district maintenance supervisor who was ordered to pay $3.4 million in connection with a fraud scheme involving federal funds. This case demonstrates continued OIG focus on K-12 fraud, particularly in facilities and operations areas where internal controls may be less stringent than in instructional programs. The supervisor had previously pleaded guilty in April 2025.

TLF Interpretation
The Boone County Schools sentencing illustrates that fraud risk exists at every level of federal program administration — not just central office. A maintenance supervisor, not an administrator, executed a $3.4 million scheme involving federal funds. This case signals that OIG actively investigates and prosecutes fraud in facilities and operations areas. Districts must ensure that robust internal controls cover all federal fund expenditures, including maintenance contracts, purchase orders, and vendor payments, with the same segregation of duties and approval requirements applied to instructional program expenditures.
Your Action
  • Review internal controls for maintenance and facilities expenditures funded with federal dollars. Confirm segregation of duties is in place — the person approving a purchase should not also receive the goods or process payment.
  • Verify that all federal procurement in maintenance and facilities follows Uniform Guidance competitive procedures (2 CFR §200.317–200.327). Sole-source procurements must be documented with specific justification.
  • Conduct or schedule a supervisory review of recent maintenance expenditures charged to federal programs. High-dollar, low-visibility procurement is a primary fraud risk area in K-12 federal programs.
  • Brief your facilities director and CFO on this case. The pattern here — extended fraud by a single employee without detection — is preventable with consistent supervisory review of expenditure documentation.
Source: Uniform Guidance procurement standards (2 CFR §200.317–200.327) · 18 U.S.C. §666 (theft from programs receiving federal funds)
Note: The November 20, 2025 sentencing announcement is confirmed on the OIG news page. Verify the specific press release at OIG news and updates ↗ by searching for Boone County Schools.

Federal Register Notice
FIPSE Special Projects: $167 Million for AI in Education, Civil Discourse, Accreditation Reform, and Short-Term Programs
U.S. Department of Education, Office of Postsecondary Education · November 12, 2025 · Application deadline: December 3, 2025 (closed)
What it says

ED published a Federal Register notice for FY 2025 FIPSE Special Projects grants totaling $167 million across four priority areas: $50 million for advancing AI in postsecondary education, $60 million for promoting civil discourse on campuses, $7 million for accreditation reform, and $50 million for capacity-building for high-quality short-term programs. The competition is open to institutions of higher education and nonprofit organizations. The application deadline was December 3, 2025. This competition is focused on postsecondary institutions. However, Absolute Priority 2 explicitly invites partnerships with SEAs and LEAs for AI literacy in K-12 and dual enrollment AI coursework.

TLF Interpretation
The FIPSE competition has closed for FY 2025, but its priority areas signal federal grant competition direction that will likely influence future K-12 programs. Notably, Absolute Priority 2 explicitly includes partnerships with SEAs and LEAs for AI literacy in K-12 and dual enrollment opportunities — districts that build AI literacy infrastructure now will be better positioned as sub-recipients or partners in future competitions. Federal programs directors should also note that the $50 million Workforce Pell-aligned short-term program priority has direct implications for Perkins-funded CTE alignment, as these programs must meet Workforce Pell eligibility requirements under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act.
Your Action
  • The December 3, 2025 deadline has passed. If your district was approached as a potential K-12 partner by a postsecondary institution applying under this competition, confirm whether any partnership agreement was formalized and whether your district has sub-recipient obligations.
  • Review your current AI literacy programming against the federal priorities described in Absolute Priorities 1 and 2. Districts that can demonstrate existing AI literacy infrastructure are better positioned as future sub-recipients or partners in federal competitions.
  • Brief your CTE director on the short-term program priority and Workforce Pell alignment requirements. Future Perkins funding discussions may reference these priority areas as an alignment framework.
  • Monitor for future K-12 grant competitions that incorporate these AI literacy and workforce alignment priorities — the federal signal here is clear and likely to recur.
Source: Federal Register, 90 FR 50861, November 12, 2025 ↗ · HEA Section 744 (20 U.S.C. §1138) · Executive Order 14179 (AI leadership) · Executive Order 14277 (AI education for youth) · One Big Beautiful Bill Act, Workforce Pell provisions

Federal Register Notice
Agency Information Collection: National Public Education Financial Survey (NPEFS) 2025–2027 Extension
U.S. Department of Education, Institute of Education Sciences · November 25, 2025
What it says

ED proposed extension of the NPEFS data collection for 2025–2027 without change. NPEFS is an annual collection of state-level finance data administered by the U.S. Census Bureau for NCES under interagency agreement, covering revenues, expenditures, and average daily attendance for all publicly funded elementary and secondary schools. The data is used to calculate each state's average per-pupil expenditure, which in turn is used to calculate federal program allocations including Title I, Part A, Impact Aid, and Indian Education. The comment period closed December 26, 2025.

TLF Interpretation
The NPEFS extension without change maintains existing data collection requirements. The critical compliance implication is one that many districts overlook: NPEFS data, which originates from state submissions compiled from district-level financial reports, is used directly to calculate Title I, Part A allocations. Inaccurate district financial reporting to the state affects not only the district's own allocation but the state's overall SPPE calculation. Districts must ensure that expenditure coding by function and fund source is accurate and consistent, as errors in district financial data flow upstream into federal allocation formulas.
Your Action
  • Review your district's financial reporting practices for accuracy in classifying expenditures by function — instruction, student support, administration, and operations — across all funding sources. Misclassification at the district level affects state NPEFS submissions.
  • Confirm your chart of accounts separates federal, state, and local revenue sources clearly and that expenditures are coded to the correct fund source. This is a prerequisite for accurate NPEFS data flow to the state.
  • Verify your district's fiscal year data submissions to the state are complete and accurate. If you are aware of prior errors or inconsistencies, contact your state education agency to determine whether resubmission is possible.
  • Brief your business official on the NPEFS-to-Title-I connection. The accuracy of financial coding is a compliance issue — not just an accounting preference — because it affects federal allocation calculations.
Source: Federal Register, 90 FR 53278, November 25, 2025 ↗ · Education Sciences Reform Act of 2002 §153(a)(1)(I) (20 U.S.C. §9543) · ESEA §8101 (SPPE definition) · OMB Control Number 1850-0067
