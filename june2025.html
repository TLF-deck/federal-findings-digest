Dear Colleague Letter
Parental Choice in Identified Schools: States May Prioritize School Improvement Funds for Choice-Offering Districts
Office of Elementary and Secondary Education · June 26, 2025
What it says

OESE sent a Dear Colleague Letter to chief state school officers on using Title I school improvement funds under ESEA section 1003(a) to support parental choice. States generally must reserve seven percent of their Title I, Part A allocation for school improvement in schools identified for Comprehensive Support and Improvement (CSI), Targeted Support and Improvement (TSI), or Additional Targeted Support and Improvement (ATSI). While students in identified schools are not required by law to be given a school choice option, states may prioritize 1003(a) funds to LEAs that both turn around underperforming schools and offer students a choice option, such as dual enrollment, academic tutoring, or career and technical education alternatives. This is the third in the administration's series of school choice guidance letters under ESEA.

TLF Interpretation
This DCL provides states discretion to favor LEAs offering parental choice when distributing 1003(a) school improvement funds. For districts with identified schools, this signals that state school improvement grant competitions may increasingly weight choice options in scoring criteria. Districts with identified schools should review their improvement plans to determine whether they include any parent-facing choice components (menus of intervention services, dual enrollment, tutoring options) that could strengthen competitiveness for 1003(a) funding. Districts that receive 1003(a) subgrants should also confirm that improvement plan activities remain allowable and that any choice-related additions are consistent with the plan's overall theory of action and stakeholder consultation requirements.
Your Action
  • Review current improvement plans for any schools identified as CSI, TSI, or ATSI. Identify whether any parent-facing choice or service menu components are already included or could be added without disrupting the plan's evidence base.
  • Contact your SEA to determine whether this guidance has affected or will affect the state's 1003(a) application scoring criteria or priority rubric for the next grant cycle.
  • If your state adopts a choice preference in its 1003(a) competition, ensure any added choice components are developed in consultation with required stakeholders (parents, teachers, principals) as required under ESEA section 1003(b).
  • Document any choice options already available to students in identified schools, including public transfer rights, supplemental services, or extracurricular alternatives. Existing documentation strengthens future applications without requiring new program design.

Federal Register Notice
American History and Civics Education Seminars Grant Competition: $14.2 Million for America's 250th
Office of Elementary and Secondary Education · June 23, 2025 · Application deadline: July 23, 2025 (closed) · 85 awards made totaling over $153 million
What it says

ED published a Federal Register notice for the first competition for the American History and Civics Education National Activities: Seminars for America's Semiquincentennial program under ESEA section 2233 (Title II, Part B). The notice anticipated approximately $14.2 million for 5-10 awards of $500,000 to $1 million per year, with a project period up to 36 months. The absolute priority required seminars for educators or students focused on American history and civics to commemorate the 250th anniversary of the United States founding. A competitive preference priority favored institutions of higher education with independent academic units dedicated to civic thought, constitutional studies, or American history. The application deadline was July 23, 2025. ED ultimately made 85 awards totaling over $153 million, substantially exceeding the original estimate.

TLF Interpretation
This grant is now closed, but the program and its priorities have ongoing relevance. The 85 awards made signal strong federal investment in patriotic history and civics education through the Semiquincentennial period. K-12 districts with existing partnerships with higher education institutions that received awards may have access to seminar content and professional development through those partners. The absolute priority's focus on the American political tradition and founding documents reflects the administration's broader supplemental grant priorities framework and signals the direction of future ED discretionary competitions involving history and civics education.
Your Action
  • The July 23, 2025 application deadline has passed. If your district applied as a partner with a higher education institution, confirm whether your institution received an award and what partnership obligations, if any, apply to your district.
  • Review the list of AHC-Seminars award recipients once published on ED's website. If a higher education institution in your region received an award, contact them about potential partnership opportunities for professional development or student programming.
  • Note the absolute priority language emphasizing founding documents and constitutional principles. This framing will appear in future ED discretionary competitions involving civics and social studies education, and should inform how districts describe their history and civics programming in future grant applications.
Source: Federal Register, 90 FR 26563, June 23, 2025 ↗ · ESEA section 2233 (20 U.S.C. §6663) · Award announcement ↗

Federal Register Notice
Educational Technology, Media, and Materials Grant Competition: National Center for Faculty Capacity in Special Education Technology
Office of Special Education and Rehabilitative Services · June 25, 2025 · Application deadline: July 25, 2025 (closed)
What it says

OSERS published a Federal Register notice for the FY 2025 competition for Educational Technology, Media, and Materials for Individuals with Disabilities grants under IDEA, specifically for a National Center to Improve Faculty Capacity to Use Educational Technology in Special Education Personnel and Leadership Preparation Programs. The program targets higher education institutions that prepare special education personnel, with a pre-application webinar held by June 30, 2025 and applications due July 25, 2025. This grant is designed for institutions of higher education preparing special education teachers and leaders, not for direct K-12 district applicants.

TLF Interpretation
This grant targets higher education institutions, not K-12 districts directly. However, districts with existing or potential partnerships with university special education preparation programs should be aware that award recipients may develop technology-integration training resources that flow to the field through pre-service preparation. The July 25, 2025 deadline has passed. Districts that partner with local universities on special education teacher preparation should inquire whether their partner institution applied and, if awarded, what resources or training opportunities may be available through the partnership.
Your Action
  • The July 25, 2025 deadline has passed. If your district has a partnership with a higher education special education preparation program, ask whether the program applied and received funding under this competition.
  • If a partner institution received an award, request information about professional development or technology resources that may become available to district cooperating teachers, mentor teachers, or special education staff through the partnership.
  • Monitor OSEP's website for grant award announcements and identify recipients that may have regional relevance to your district.

Investigation Opened
OCR Opens Title IX Investigation into Jefferson County Public Schools (CO) on Sex-Separated Facilities and Athletics
Office for Civil Rights · Investigation opened June 2025 · Findings and proposed resolution issued March 13, 2026
Timeline note: The investigation into Jefferson County Public Schools opened in June 2025 over allegations involving single-sex overnight accommodations on school trips. OCR's findings and proposed resolution agreement were not announced until March 13, 2026, outside the June 2025 window of this issue. The findings are covered in the December 2025 issue of this digest. This entry addresses the investigation opening and the compliance signal it sends to all districts.
What it says

OCR opened a Title IX directed investigation into Jefferson County Public Schools in Colorado in June 2025, initially focused on allegations that the district had removed single-sex overnight accommodation safeguards on district-sponsored trips. The investigation expanded in scope. When OCR announced findings on March 13, 2026, it concluded the district violated Title IX by allowing biological male students access to female bathrooms, locker rooms, and overnight accommodations and to compete on girls sports teams. OCR issued a proposed resolution agreement giving the district 10 days to voluntarily comply or face enforcement action. Jefferson County is the second-largest school district in Colorado, and the investigation is one of over a dozen similar OCR Title IX probes launched under the current administration.

TLF Interpretation
The opening of this investigation in June 2025 signals the administration's enforcement posture on Title IX facility access and athletic eligibility questions for districts nationwide. OCR is conducting at least 18 similar investigations. Districts should treat the June 2025 investigation opening as the compliance signal, even though the findings came later. The Jefferson County case involves policies governing restroom access, locker room use, overnight accommodations, and athletic team eligibility for transgender students, all under the 2020 Title IX regulations currently in effect. Jefferson County disputes OCR's interpretation and notes that its policies comply with Colorado law. Districts in states with similar state nondiscrimination laws face the same federal-state tension.
Your Action
  • Review your district's current policies governing sex-separated facility access (restrooms, locker rooms), overnight accommodations on school trips, and athletic team eligibility. Confirm which legal framework each policy references.
  • If your district's policies are grounded in prior Title IX guidance or state nondiscrimination law that conflicts with the 2020 Title IX regulations, consult district legal counsel now, before an investigation is opened. The Jefferson County case illustrates that OCR will not delay findings based on state law conflict.
  • Document the legal basis for each relevant policy. Districts should be able to articulate why each policy is legally defensible under currently enforceable law, including the 2020 Title IX regulations, state law, and any applicable court orders in your jurisdiction.
  • Brief your Title IX coordinator, athletic director, and principal leadership on the current Title IX enforcement environment. Staff should understand that OCR is actively investigating these policy areas and that the district's response to complaints in this area will be scrutinized.
Sources: Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972 (20 U.S.C. §1681) · 2020 Title IX regulations (34 CFR Part 106) · ED press release on findings, March 13, 2026 ↗
Note: The investigation opened June 2025. The OCR findings and proposed resolution agreement were issued March 13, 2026. The case number in the original source document (03145001) refers to a separate, earlier Title VI racial discrimination agreement with Jefferson County Public Schools and is not the source for this Title IX entry.

Fraud Advisory
Fraud Advisory: FAFSA Activity Involving Dependent Students with Unusual Parent Data
Office of Inspector General · June 2025
What it says

OIG initiated analysis of additional data in June 2025 and found that the pattern of fraudulent activity involving dependent students with unusual parent data on FAFSA applications had continued since it was first identified in March 2025. The fraud pattern involves suspicious parent information on dependent student FAFSA forms that may indicate identity theft or manipulation of financial aid eligibility data. While this primarily affects postsecondary financial aid, the pattern directly affects high school students completing FAFSA applications, and districts running college access programs including Federal TRIO and Gear Up have a role in advising students through the application process.

TLF Interpretation
The June 2025 continuation of this fraud pattern signals an ongoing and organized effort to exploit dependent student FAFSA submissions. K-12 districts running TRIO, Gear Up, or other college access programs that include FAFSA assistance should ensure counselors and advisors are trained to recognize red flags. The most common indicators are parent financial information that appears inconsistent with what families have shared, unusual tax filing statuses, or parent income figures that seem implausible. Districts are not responsible for preventing this fraud at the federal system level, but counselors who assist students with FAFSA completion can play a role in alerting students and families to protect their information.
Your Action
  • Brief school counselors and TRIO/Gear Up staff on the FAFSA fraud pattern. Train them to advise students to safeguard parent Social Security numbers, tax information, and FSA ID credentials and never share these with third parties at FAFSA events.
  • Review protocols for district-run FAFSA completion events. Confirm that staff assisting with FAFSA completion are not accessing parent credentials on students' behalf and that students are entering their own and parents' information directly.
  • If any student or family reports that their FAFSA data appears to have been tampered with or that they received unexpected financial aid determinations, advise them to contact Federal Student Aid's fraud reporting line at 1-800-MIS-USED and flag the situation in your TRIO or Gear Up program documentation.
Source: OIG News and Updates ↗ · OIG Fraud Advisory on FAFSA Dependent Student Activity, June 2025
Note: The specific June 2025 Fraud Advisory update is documented in the OIG's FY 2025 semiannual report and fraud advisory series. Verify the current advisory at the OIG news page linked above.

Impact Aid
Payment Announcement
FY 2025 Final Impact Aid Payments Released: Earliest in Program History
Office of Elementary and Secondary Education, Impact Aid Program · June 5, 2025
What it says

ED announced that as of June 5, 2025, it had released the final payments for all sections of Impact Aid: section 7003(b) Basic Support Payments, section 7003(d) Children with Disabilities, section 7002 Federal Property, and section 7007(a) Formula Construction, all for FY 2025. This is the earliest ED has ever released final payments for any fiscal year. Section 7003(b) Basic Support payments were made at a final rate of 94.64 percent of the maximum payment amount.

TLF Interpretation
The early release of final Impact Aid payments is operationally significant for districts that rely on this funding for budget planning. Receiving final payments in June rather than later in the fiscal year allows for more accurate budget reconciliation before fiscal year close and reduces the need to budget conservatively based on estimated rates. Districts should verify receipt of all four payment categories, confirm the amounts against their approved applications, and update budget projections to reflect the 94.64 percent final rate for section 7003(b), which is below full funding and affects districts differently depending on their eligible student populations.
Your Action
  • Confirm receipt of FY 2025 final payments for each applicable Impact Aid section: 7003(b) Basic Support, 7003(d) Children with Disabilities, 7002 Federal Property, and 7007(a) Formula Construction. Contact the Impact Aid Program if any expected payment has not been received.
  • Reconcile final payment amounts against approved application amounts and the 94.64 percent final rate for section 7003(b). Update your district's FY 2025 budget to reflect actual rather than estimated Impact Aid revenue.
  • Document the final rate and payment date for audit purposes. If your district's budget was built on a different estimated rate, document the variance and its effect on program expenditures.
  • Begin preparing FY 2026 Impact Aid applications with the FY 2025 final rate as a reference point for conservative revenue projections, noting that final rates can vary year to year based on appropriations levels.
Source: Impact Aid Program: June 2025 News You Can Use ↗ · Impact Aid Program, ESEA sections 7001-7015 (20 U.S.C. §7701 et seq.)
