Letter to Chief State School Officers
OESE Letter: States May Use Up to 3% of Title I Allocation for Direct Student Services Supporting Education Choice
Office of Elementary and Secondary Education · March 31, 2025 · Signed by Hayley Sanon, Principal Deputy/Acting Assistant Secretary
What it says

The Department's Office of Elementary and Secondary Education sent a letter to all chief state school officers on March 31, 2025, highlighting existing flexibility under ESEA section 1003A that allows a state to reserve up to 3 percent of its Title I, Part A allocation to provide funds to LEAs for direct student services. The letter frames this flexibility as a tool to support education choice initiatives, implementing President Trump's Executive Order on Expanding Educational Freedom and Opportunity for Families (January 29, 2025). The letter notes that Ohio is currently the only state using this section 1003A flexibility. Under this provision, funds may be used for credit recovery, advanced coursework, academic tutoring, dual enrollment, career and technical education, and other services that allow parents to exercise meaningful choice in their child's education. The letter is the first in a series of guidance documents the Department signaled it would issue on education choice options within existing ESEA flexibilities.

TLF Interpretation
This letter does not create new law or new flexibility; section 1003A has existed in ESEA since the Every Student Succeeds Act. What is new is the administration's explicit framing of this provision as an education choice tool and its signal that states are expected to explore using it. For federal programs directors, the key compliance implication is that any LEA receiving these funds must ensure that direct student services: (1) are supplemental to services otherwise available; (2) reach students attending Title I schools or students from those attendance areas who attend private schools under equitable services requirements; (3) are documented as meeting supplement-not-supplant requirements; and (4) comply with all Title I administrative and fiscal requirements. The Ohio-only uptake also signals that most SEA Title I directors are unfamiliar with this provision, making it a likely focus of forthcoming technical assistance.
Your Action
  • Check whether your SEA has adopted or is considering adopting the section 1003A direct student services flexibility. If your state does adopt it, review what services are allowable under the provision and confirm your district's Title I coordinator understands the supplement-not-supplant and equitable services implications before accepting any funds.
  • If your district currently offers credit recovery, dual enrollment, tutoring, or CTE pathways funded through Title I, confirm those expenditures are correctly documented under the applicable ESEA authority. Do not retroactively reclassify existing expenditures as direct student services without reviewing the specific section 1003A requirements and your SEA's guidance.
  • Monitor your SEA for follow-up communications responding to this letter. Because Ohio was the only state using this flexibility at the time of issuance, many SEAs had not previously developed procedures for it. Your state may issue implementing guidance or application processes in the months following this letter.
  • Review the broader series of education choice letters ED signaled it would issue. The March 31 letter is the first; subsequent letters covering persistently dangerous school choice options, school improvement provisions, and equitable services were issued through August 2025. Each has Title I compliance implications.
Sources: ED Press Release, March 31, 2025 ↗ · ESEA section 1003A (20 U.S.C. 6303a) · Executive Order, Expanding Educational Freedom and Opportunity for Families (Jan. 29, 2025)

Investigation Opened (March 2025); Findings Issued March 18, 2026
D.C. Public Schools: OCR Opens Directed Investigation for Section 504 and ADA Violations in Disability Services
Office for Civil Rights · Investigation opened March 2025 · Noncompliance finding and proposed resolution: March 18, 2026
Status Update: Findings issued March 18, 2026. OCR opened this directed investigation in March 2025 and concluded it approximately one year later. On March 18, 2026, OCR announced that DCPS violated Section 504 and Title II by denying students with disabilities timely evaluations, individualized placements, and reliable transportation, finding that "DCPS has no way to ensure students with disabilities have adequate transportation to and from school, presenting serious safety concerns." OCR issued a proposed resolution agreement; as of March 18, 2026, DCPS had not yet accepted it. If not accepted, OCR may initiate enforcement action.
What it says

In March 2025, OCR initiated a directed investigation into D.C. Public Schools regarding allegations that DCPS was discriminating against students with disabilities in programs receiving federal financial assistance, in violation of Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and Title II of the ADA. The investigation followed a December 2024 report from the D.C. Advisory Committee to the U.S. Commission on Civil Rights finding that DCPS had the highest rate of IDEA due process complaints per 10,000 students served of any state or territory, and that the district's dispute resolution system placed an undue burden on families to demand services. Transportation for students with disabilities, including long delays, unreliable schedules, and lax oversight, was a specific concern identified in both the Advisory Committee report and a March 2024 federal class action complaint. OCR's investigation, concluded in March 2026, found systemic violations across evaluation timelines, individualized placement decisions, and transportation reliability. OCR proposed a resolution agreement requiring DCPS to establish a Disability Services Division, revise all disability-related policies, conduct annual training for district and transportation staff, and develop a transportation management process.

TLF Interpretation
The DCPS case is a directed compliance review, not a complaint-driven investigation. That distinction matters for other districts: OCR selected DCPS based on data and prior reports, without a specific individual complaint triggering the review. The three violation categories here (evaluation timelines, individualized placement decisions, and transportation) represent the most operationally common Section 504 compliance gaps in large urban districts. The transportation finding is particularly notable: OCR found not merely delays but a systemic inability to ensure safe transportation, which it characterized as presenting "serious safety concerns." For any district with a significant transportation operation serving students with disabilities, this case signals that transportation reliability is within OCR's Section 504 enforcement scope, not merely an operations matter. The proposed requirement for a dedicated Disability Services Division also illustrates OCR's willingness to require structural organizational changes, not just policy revisions.
Your Action
  • Audit your district's Section 504 evaluation timelines. The standard under EDGAR and most state regulations is a reasonable timeline after referral; many districts set this at 60 days. Review your actual completion times against your written policy and identify any backlog, particularly for students referred but not yet evaluated.
  • Review your transportation protocols for students with disabilities specifically. Confirm that your district has a written process for managing bus delays, cancellations, and safety incidents for students with IEPs or 504 plans, and that accountability for these issues is assigned to a named staff role. This is the finding category most likely to be overlooked in routine Section 504 compliance audits.
  • Confirm your district has a mechanism for tracking and reporting Section 504 and IDEA due process complaints. OCR used DCPS's unusually high due process rate as part of its basis for opening the directed review. Districts with elevated complaint rates are at elevated risk of directed investigations.
  • Assess whether your district's organizational structure assigns clear accountability for Section 504 compliance across evaluation, placement, and related services. The proposed Disability Services Division in the DCPS resolution is a signal that OCR views diffuse accountability as a systemic gap, not just a policy problem.
Sources: OCR Press Release, March 18, 2026 ↗ · Section 504, Rehabilitation Act (29 U.S.C. 794) · Title II, ADA (42 U.S.C. 12132) · 34 CFR Part 104
The investigation was opened in March 2025 (the period covered by this digest). The noncompliance findings and proposed resolution agreement were issued March 18, 2026. This entry covers the investigation opening as the March 2025 event; the finding is presented as a status update. DCPS had not accepted the proposed resolution agreement as of the March 18, 2026 press release date.

Press Release / Reduction in Force
Department of Education Initiates Reduction in Force: Nearly 50% of Workforce Eliminated, Including 243 OCR Staff
U.S. Department of Education · March 11, 2025 · Administrative leave effective March 21, 2025 · RIF actions rescinded for OCR, January 2026
Status: OCR RIF actions rescinded January 2026. Following multiple court challenges and preliminary injunctions that blocked or paused the layoffs, the Department recalled OCR staff in December 2025. In early January 2026, the Department rescinded the RIF actions for OCR staff entirely. A GAO report (released early 2026) calculated that paying salaries and benefits to sidelined OCR staff cost between $28.5 million and $38 million from mid-March through December 2025. The RIF actions affecting other offices (FSA, IES) followed a separate litigation track.
What it says

On March 11, 2025, the Department of Education announced a reduction in force (RIF) cutting nearly 50 percent of its approximately 4,133-person workforce, describing the action as part of its "final mission." More than 1,300 positions were eliminated through the RIF, with roughly an additional 600 employees having already departed through voluntary resignation or retirement. Affected staff were notified March 11 and placed on administrative leave effective March 21, 2025. The cuts fell heavily on three offices with direct K-12 relevance: at least 243 of approximately 550 OCR staff were eliminated, closing seven of OCR's twelve regional offices (Chicago, Philadelphia, New York, Dallas, San Francisco, Boston, and Cleveland); 326 Federal Student Aid staff were cut; and IES was reduced to a skeletal staff of fewer than ten people in leadership positions, with nearly all research, data, and statistics staff eliminated. The English Language Acquisition office (OELA) was entirely eliminated. As of the March layoffs, OCR had over 6,000 open investigations.

TLF Interpretation
The scale of this RIF is without historical precedent at the Department of Education. For K-12 districts, the immediate operational implication was the loss of most OCR regional office capacity: seven of twelve regional offices closed, taking with them the attorneys and investigators responsible for resolving open complaints and conducting compliance reviews. Districts with pending OCR matters faced extended timelines and, in some cases, no active assigned investigator. The elimination of OELA is directly relevant to Title III and English learner programs: OELA produced technical assistance, guidance, and monitoring for EL programs, and its elimination left SEAs and LEAs without a federal point of contact for EL policy questions. The near-total elimination of IES affects NCES data collection, including the Common Core of Data and other LEA-level data systems. However, court challenges ultimately forced partial recall and then rescission of the OCR RIFs; the longer-term operational impact on other offices remains unclear and warrants continued monitoring.
Your Action
  • If your district has a pending OCR complaint or investigation, contact your assigned OCR regional office to confirm case status and current contact information. The seven closed offices were consolidated into remaining offices; reassignment of open cases was not uniformly communicated. Do not assume an absence of contact means a case is closed.
  • Do not treat reduced federal oversight as reducing your district's legal obligations. Section 504, Title VI, Title IX, IDEA, Title I, and all other federal statutes remain in full force. A reduction in enforcement capacity does not change compliance requirements or limit the availability of private legal actions by affected students and families.
  • If your district relies on OELA for technical assistance on Title III or English learner program compliance, redirect inquiries to your SEA Title III director. With OELA eliminated, SEAs are the primary point of contact for EL program questions, and some SEAs have built internal capacity while others have not.
  • Note that OCR RIF actions were rescinded in January 2026, and OCR regional offices are being reconstituted. Monitor OCR's website and the regional office directory for updates on office openings, contact information changes, and any renewed outreach on previously dormant cases.
Sources: ED Press Release, March 11, 2025 ↗ · GAO Report GAO-26-108320: ED OCR RIF Costs and Savings ↗

OIG Semiannual Report to Congress, No. 90: October 1, 2024 through March 31, 2025
Office of Inspector General · Reporting period: October 1, 2024 to March 31, 2025
What it says

The OIG's statutorily required semiannual report to Congress (No. 90) covers the period October 1, 2024 through March 31, 2025, presenting audits, investigations, and oversight activities conducted during that period to identify fraud, waste, and abuse in Department of Education programs. The report covers OIG work across federal student aid programs (including FAFSA fraud patterns), program oversight, and Departmental financial compliance. This reporting period coincides with the RIF and restructuring of the Department, and the report was submitted against the backdrop of the March 2025 workforce reductions. The OIG operates independently of the Department's leadership and its reporting obligations are statutory; the OIG was not subject to the March 2025 RIF that affected program and enforcement offices.

TLF Interpretation
OIG semiannual reports are an important compliance reference for federal programs directors because they document the audit findings, investigation patterns, and enforcement priorities that OIG uses when selecting targets for future audits and referrals. Patterns identified in OIG's work, including procurement fraud, improper cost allocations, subgrant monitoring failures, and untimely drawdowns, often signal the criteria OIG will apply in future LEA-level audits or single audit reviews. Federal programs directors should review the report directly and identify any K-12 or formula grant findings that parallel their own program areas. The OIG remained operationally intact through the March 2025 RIF period, which means its oversight activities and referral capacity were unaffected by the departmental restructuring.
Your Action
  • Download and review Semiannual Report No. 90 directly from oversight.gov or oig.ed.gov. Review the audit findings sections for any K-12 formula grant program areas (Title I, IDEA, Title III, Perkins) for patterns relevant to your district's fiscal operations.
  • Note any OIG findings on subgrant monitoring, procurement, or cost documentation in the report. These are consistently among the highest-risk areas for LEAs in single audits and are the categories most likely to appear in future OIG or single audit findings.
  • If your district has received a federal subgrant from your SEA or another pass-through entity, review your subgrant monitoring documentation. OIG repeatedly identifies pass-through entity monitoring failures as a systemic weakness, and LEAs can be implicated in findings even when they are not the direct auditee.
  • Use the OIG report in conjunction with your single audit results to conduct a self-assessment of compliance risk. Overlap between OIG-identified risk patterns and your district's own audit findings is a signal to prioritize those areas for corrective action.
