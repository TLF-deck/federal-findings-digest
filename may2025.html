Federal Context: May 2025
OCR
Federal Context
OCR Enforcement Effectively Suspended: March through September 2025
U.S. Department of Education, Office for Civil Rights · March 2025 through September 2025
What happened

In March 2025, the Trump administration placed the majority of OCR staff on administrative leave as part of a broader reduction in force. A federal court blocked the layoffs, but staff remained on paid leave and were not performing enforcement work. Between March and September 2025, OCR received 9,269 complaints and resolved 7,072, with approximately 90% resolved through dismissal rather than investigation (figures from a February 2026 GAO report, as documented in the December 2025 issue of this digest). The total number of OCR resolution agreements for all of 2025 was substantially lower than prior years; secondary sources estimate approximately 177 agreements in 2025 compared to 518 in 2024, though these figures have not been verified against a primary source. In December 2025, ED recalled OCR staff, and in January 2026 rescinded the reduction in force.

TLF Interpretation
The suspension of OCR enforcement activity does not suspend district compliance obligations. Title VI, Title IX, Section 504, and IDEA civil rights requirements remain in force regardless of OCR's operational capacity. Districts should treat this period as an opportunity to conduct internal compliance reviews, update grievance procedures, and ensure documentation is audit-ready. When OCR enforcement resumes at full capacity, cases that accumulated during the suspension period will require resolution. Districts that deferred compliance work during this window carry elevated risk when enforcement normalizes.
Your Action
  • Do not treat reduced OCR enforcement as a compliance holiday. Obligations under Title VI, Title IX, Section 504, and IDEA continue. Private rights of action remain available to affected students and families independent of OCR enforcement activity.
  • Conduct an internal review of your district's grievance procedures for civil rights complaints. Confirm procedures are current, that designated coordinators are trained, and that response timelines are being met even when OCR is not actively monitoring.
  • Document any civil rights complaints received by the district during this period and the district's response. This documentation protects the district if those complaints are later referred to OCR after enforcement resumes.
  • Review any OCR complaints your district filed as a complainant during this period. If complaints were dismissed without investigation, evaluate whether refiling is warranted now that OCR is fully staffed.
Source: GAO report on OCR operations, February 2026 (for complaint statistics) · Title VI (42 U.S.C. §2000d) · Title IX (20 U.S.C. §1681) · Section 504 (29 U.S.C. §794)
Note: The complaint volume and resolution statistics are from a GAO report published February 2026, as documented in the December 2025 issue of this digest. The 177 vs. 518 agreement comparison is from secondary reporting and has not been verified against a primary OCR source.
DEI / Title VI
Court Orders
DEI Dear Colleague Letter Blocked by Federal Courts; Subsequently Vacated
Multiple federal district courts · April 24, 2025 (injunctions) · Later vacated in 2025
What happened

On February 14, 2025, OCR issued a Dear Colleague Letter asserting that many DEI programs constitute unlawful racial discrimination under Title VI and threatening loss of federal funding for noncompliance. On April 3, 2025, the Department required states and school districts to certify compliance by April 24. Three federal courts issued orders on April 24, 2025. The Maryland court (AFT v. DOE) issued a nationwide stay of the DCL under the Administrative Procedure Act. The New Hampshire court (NEA v. DOE) issued a preliminary injunction limited to the plaintiff organizations and their member entities, finding the DCL likely unconstitutional on First Amendment, Fifth Amendment, and APA grounds. The DC court (NAACP v. DOE) issued a limited injunction on the certification requirement only. As of May 2025, the DCL had no enforceable legal effect. The DCL was subsequently vacated entirely by a federal court later in 2025, as described in later issues of this digest.

TLF Interpretation
In May 2025, the DCL had no legal enforcement effect due to the injunctions. However, districts that had already modified equity programs in response to the February letter were operating in a legally unsettled environment. The injunctions were limited to plaintiffs and their members in the NH case, and the legal landscape remained in flux. Districts that changed programming in response to the DCL should have documented those changes with legal review notes indicating the basis for any modifications. The Department continued to assert its underlying legal position even while enjoined, and the risk of future enforcement action if injunctions were lifted required ongoing legal monitoring throughout 2025.
Your Action
  • Confirm that your district's legal counsel has reviewed the current status of the DEI DCL and the subsequent vacatur. The DCL was ultimately vacated in 2025; however, the administration's underlying Title VI enforcement posture has not changed and continues to affect districts through other enforcement mechanisms.
  • Document any program modifications made in response to the February 14 DCL, including the legal basis for each change, who made the decision, and the date. This documentation is essential if changes are challenged or if the district is audited on program compliance.
  • Ensure your district's Title VI grievance procedure and civil rights coordinator designation remain current and functional regardless of the DCL's status. Title VI compliance obligations are statutory and independent of the DCL.
  • Brief your district counsel on the full timeline: February 14 DCL, April 24 injunctions, subsequent vacatur. Any district program reviews should be based on current enforceable law, not on the vacated guidance.
Sources: AFT v. DOE, D. Maryland, April 24, 2025 (nationwide stay) · NEA v. DOE, D.N.H., April 24, 2025 (preliminary injunction) · NAACP v. DOE, D.D.C., April 24, 2025 (certification injunction) · Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (42 U.S.C. §2000d)
Note: The DCL was subsequently vacated by a federal court later in 2025. Districts should confirm the current legal status of DEI guidance with district counsel, as enforcement activity and legal developments have continued since May 2025.
Title I / ESEA
Dear Colleague Letter
Unsafe School Choice Option: States Must Offer Transfer Rights for Students in Persistently Dangerous Schools
Office of Elementary and Secondary Education · May 7, 2025
What it says

OESE sent a Dear Colleague Letter to chief state school officers clarifying the Unsafe School Choice Option under ESEA section 8532. The letter reaffirms that states receiving ESEA funds must maintain statewide policies allowing students to transfer to a safe school when: (1) the student attends a school the state has determined to be persistently dangerous based on state-defined criteria, or (2) the student becomes a victim of a violent criminal offense while at school. The letter emphasizes the wide latitude states have in defining "persistently dangerous" and notes that the number of schools nationally designated as persistently dangerous (25 in 2023-24, zero in 2021-22) appears inconsistently low relative to the 1.2 million violent offenses reported in the CRDC for the same period. States are encouraged to build robust communication protocols and leverage open enrollment policies to satisfy the provision.

TLF Interpretation
This letter reaffirms existing ESEA requirements rather than creating new obligations, but it signals the Department's intent to scrutinize state and district implementation of the Unsafe School Choice provision. The explicit comparison between reported violent offenses and persistently dangerous school designations signals that the Department views most states as under-designating unsafe schools. Districts should confirm their state has a current, operational definition of persistently dangerous schools and that the district's safety data is being collected and reported to the state accurately. If the state has not designated any schools as persistently dangerous, districts should understand the criteria and verify whether any of their schools could qualify under the state's definition.
Your Action
  • Confirm your state's current definition of "persistently dangerous school" and the annual reporting cycle. If your state has not updated its definition in several years, contact your SEA to determine whether a revision is planned in response to this letter.
  • Review your district's procedures for notifying parents if a school is designated as persistently dangerous and for offering transfer options to safe public schools within the LEA. Confirm notification timelines and transfer logistics are documented.
  • Verify that your district's safety data is being accurately collected and reported through the state to meet ESEA section 8532 requirements. Inaccurate or incomplete safety data reporting affects both state designation decisions and federal compliance.
  • For students who have been victims of violent criminal offenses at school, confirm your district has a documented protocol for offering the required transfer option, separate from the persistently dangerous school designation process.
